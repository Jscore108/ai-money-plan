§ VIII · Tricks & Plays
Sharp Edges
Specific tactics, not vague advice
01

Price change orders using historical labor ratios, not gut feel

Feed Claude the original scope labor hours + the new scope. Let it compute the proportional labor delta before you apply productivity factors. Catches under-pricing on the labor side, which is where most COs lose money.

Angle 01
02

Write RFIs that force billable answers

Structure every RFI with three parts: (1) what the drawings show, (2) what the field found, (3) the specific question. Vague RFIs get vague answers. Precise RFIs surface scope changes that become COs.

Angle 01
03

Treat submittal reviews as a spec-diff problem

Submittal comes in. Contract spec goes in. Ask AI for the diff — not a summary. What does the submittal NOT cover that the spec requires? That's the comment list that protects scope.

Angle 01
04

Two-tier markup in every CO

Keep direct job costs and overhead-applied costs visibly separate in line items. GCs and owners challenge markup differently when it's a clean line vs. rolled into unit prices.

Angle 01
05

Always include delay/disruption language in CO proposals

Even when no delay is known yet. Preserves the right to claim later. Standard reservation-of-rights clause in every template.

Angle 01
06

Log trading ideas before entering, review before exiting

The 3-axis journal (asset / source / theme) only works if ideas are logged with a thesis before capital is committed. AI's job is synthesis at the weekly review, not signal generation day-of.

Angle 02
07

Layer AI on top of Arch Public — don't replace it

Arch Public handles execution discipline you already paid for. AI handles the thinking Arch doesn't do: macro context, regime detection, thesis-vs-reality audits. Use both.

Angle 02
08

Paper-trade with real position sizes, not fantasy sizes

If you're paper-trading with $10M when your real capital is $10k, the psychology doesn't transfer. Use the sizes you'd actually use. Otherwise the learning is worthless.

Angle 02
09

Sell outcomes to owners, not tech

Owners don't care about MCP or Claude Code. They care about "we priced 20% more COs accurately this quarter" and "our average RFI turnaround dropped from 8 days to 3." Translate every feature into a P&L impact before the conversation.

Angle 03
10

Get adoption before you ask for the raise

3+ colleagues actively using the toolkit on real projects > a working demo you used alone. Owners pay more for leverage across the team, not for a side project. No adoption = no ask. Fix adoption first.

Angle 03
11

If you productize, sell to people exactly like you

Mid-size industrial electrical subs. Union IBEW shops. Water/wastewater + industrial focus. Not "all contractors." Narrower ICP = faster sale. Your credibility in that niche is the moat.

Angle 04
12

Don't build what you could buy for $20/mo

Estimating software exists. Timekeeping software exists. The gap is how AI drafts, prices, and reviews the outputs of those systems. Stay in the gap. Don't rebuild Accubid.

Angle 04
13

Domain credibility is the moat

Someone without electrical PM background can't build this product no matter how good at AI they are. That's the defensibility. Don't dilute it by trying to be generic.

Angle 04
14

Measure in dollars recovered, not hours saved

"I saved 2 hours" is unfalsifiable and forgettable. "I recovered $3,400 on CO #14" is real and compounds into a case for productizing — and into content for Angle 05.

Meta
15

Keep a failure log alongside the wins

Tools that didn't work, prompts that failed, tactics that sounded smart but flopped. Prevents re-evaluating dead ends and produces honest reviews — and much better content later.

Meta
16

Revise this document monthly, not continuously

Don't tweak this every day. Set a monthly review. Let real data accumulate between revisions. The discipline is the point.
